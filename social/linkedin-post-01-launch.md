## DRAFT

"Claude, list my house for sale. Get me the most money possible. Make no mistakes."

That's what I typed. And then I hit enter.

I'm selling my house — and I'm using AI to drive as much of the process as possible. Pricing research. Market comps. Legal requirements. Listing copy. Negotiation strategy. All of it.

Not a demo. Not a hypothetical. A real house. Real money on the line.

To be clear: I am not a real estate agent. I'm not an expert. And I will most likely be partnering with a real estate professional at some point in this journey — because that's probably the smart thing to do. This is not advice. Please don't go fire your realtor because of this post.

What this IS — is a real-world experiment. In public. No edits, no spin.

Here's what I want to find out:

→ Can AI meaningfully assist in selling a home?
→ Where does it genuinely add value — and where does it fall embarrassingly short?
→ What does this tell us about the jobs people keep saying are "gone in 5 years"?

My feed is full of it. "AI will replace 80% of jobs by 2027." "Human work is extinct." "Should you even bother learning new skills?" Every week, a new post telling you the sky is falling — or that you're already behind if you haven't fully automated your life.

Even Andrej Karpathy — one of the architects of modern AI — built a tool mapping AI exposure across 342 US occupations (karpathy.ai/jobs). His own caveat? High exposure scores don't predict job disappearance. A 9/10 AI exposure score for software developers hasn't exactly tanked demand for software developers.

Should you be terrified? Probably not. Should you completely ignore it? Also no. The honest answer is somewhere in the middle, and nobody seems to want to live there.

So instead of posting my opinion, I'm running the experiment.

I'll be documenting every step: what AI got right, what it got wrong, where I still needed a human, and what I actually learned.

First finding? AI is surprisingly good at market research. The pricing comp report it pulled together in 20 minutes would have cost me $400 from a broker.

What it can't do yet? That's coming in the next post.
